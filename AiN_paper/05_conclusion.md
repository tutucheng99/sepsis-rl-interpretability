# 5. Conclusion

This study demonstrates that offline RL algorithm selection profoundly impacts policy interpretability independent of performance in the gym-sepsis simulator. Our systematic LEG-based benchmark shows that Conservative Q-Learning achieves substantially stronger saliency magnitude than vanilla DQN, spanning multiple orders of magnitude (40.06 vs. 0.069, representing a 600-fold difference), while achieving comparable survival outcomes. While all algorithms achieve similar overall survival rates ranging from 94.0% to 95.4%, CQL substantially outperforms DQN on high-severity patients with SOFA ≥ 11 (88.5% vs. 84.3%), representing a clinically meaningful performance gap. CQL's strong, clinically coherent saliency patterns emphasizing blood pressure and lactate align with Surviving Sepsis Campaign guidelines ^4^ and provide quantitative feature importance suitable for regulatory review ^12^.

Our findings within this single simulator suggest that conservative offline RL methods warrant consideration for safety-critical healthcare applications where interpretability is essential. CQL's conservative penalty term biases the Q-function toward values supported by the training dataset, inheriting the behavioral policy's interpretable structure based on threshold-based heuristics ^17^. This interpretability-by-design mechanism offers an alternative to post-hoc explainability methods. The clinical urgency extends beyond acute survival to preventing long-term neurological sequelae, as sepsis triggers blood-brain barrier disruption, neuroinflammation, and accelerated neurodegeneration ^7,8,30^. Interpretable policies like CQL enable clinicians to validate whether treatment recommendations account for these complex pathophysiological interactions.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. Our conclusions are based on a single sepsis treatment simulator with known constraints including high baseline survival (approximately 94-95% across all policies including random) and learned dynamics that may not capture true causal treatment effects. The evaluation used independently sampled episodes rather than identical patient trajectories, precluding direct patient-level comparisons. Additionally, LEG analysis relies on local linear approximations that may not fully capture non-linear feature interactions. Claims about broader applicability to other clinical domains or real-world deployment require prospective validation through multi-center clinical trials, human-in-the-loop studies with clinicians, and evaluation on real EHR data ^35^.

Future work should pursue prospective clinical validation, integrate domain knowledge into CQL training through feature-aware regularization, develop healthcare-specific interpretability metrics including stability, parsimony, and actionability, and conduct human-in-the-loop experiments to establish causality between interpretability and clinical decision quality ^29^. By prioritizing interpretability alongside performance in algorithm design, we can develop clinical AI systems that earn the trust and adoption of clinicians while improving patient care and potentially mitigating long-term neurological sequelae in sepsis survivors.
